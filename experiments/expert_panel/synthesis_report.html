1. Executive Summary

This paper measures the quantitative relationship between weight-space divergence and Linear Mode Connectivity (LMC) barrier height when Pythia-1.4B is fine-tuned on code and medical reasoning data. All six expert reviewers independently verified the core numerical claims: the weight divergence measurements, barrier computations, and noise-floor calibrations are accurate. The experimental design — 3 seeds, bootstrap CIs, within-domain baselines, and noise-floor calibration — demonstrates genuine methodological care. However, the paper has four fatal weaknesses that prevent acceptance at NeurIPS/ICML in its current form: (1) the core "sublinear" claim rests on only two valid divergence levels, making it unsubstantiated; (2) the high-divergence medical barrier (0.228) exceeds the claimed "upper bound" (0.222), an internal contradiction; (3) n=3 seeds with 45% coefficient of variation renders the high-divergence medical result statistically unreliable; and (4) the "domain specialization" framing is weakened by the fact that the training data is PRM verification data, not genuine domain-adaptation corpora. The paper's strongest contribution — that medical within-domain training instability drives larger barriers than cross-domain difference — is genuinely novel but underdeveloped. The minimum viable paper at TMLR requires adding one model family, two domains, and resolving the upper-bound contradiction (~12 GPU-hours). The path to ICLR requires a systematic divergence sweep, permutation-alignment checks, and connection to downstream merge quality (~2–3 GPU-days).

3. Convergence Analysis

3.1 What ALL Experts Agree On

  • The numbers are correct. All three experts who independently recomputed barriers (Theorist, Experimentalist, Adversarial) confirm the paper's reported values match the raw data using the correct Frankle formula. Weight divergence measurements are verified against HuggingFace base checkpoint.
  • The within-domain medical finding is the paper's strongest contribution. All six experts independently identify the medical within-domain barrier (0.147) exceeding the cross-domain barrier (0.051) as the most novel, interesting, and defensible result in the paper.
  • n=3 seeds is insufficient for the high-divergence medical condition. With coefficient of variation = 45%, the 95% CI spans [0.094, 0.340], making the point estimate unreliable. All experts flag this.
  • The "sublinear" claim is unsubstantiated. Only two valid divergence levels exist (standard, high). The intermediate lr=2e-4/lr=3e-4 results are byte-identical file copies. A functional-form claim requires at least 3–4 distinct divergence levels. The Theorist, Adversarial Reviewer, and Meta Reviewer all flag this explicitly.
  • The high-divergence convergence confound is serious. High-divergence models have worse self-domain loss than standard-divergence models. The barrier increase cannot be cleanly attributed to weight displacement vs. under-convergence. Five experts raise this.
  • The noise-floor calibration is methodologically strong. The identical-copy (barrier ~0) and pretrained-to-random-init baselines are exemplary rigor checks. All experts praise this.
  • The barrier formula bug exists in the scan script. Both lmc_barrier_scan.py and lmc_barrier.py compute max(L) - L[0] instead of max(L) - (L[0]+L[-1])/2. The Theorist and Experimentalist independently discovered this. The paper's numbers use the correct formula (verified), but the code-paper inconsistency is a reproducibility hazard. Five experts note this.
  • The paper does not belong at ACL/EMNLP. The Domain Specialist, Meta Reviewer, and Adversarial Reviewer all conclude that the NLP contribution is near-zero and the venue mismatch guarantees negative reviews.
  • The lr=2e-4/lr=3e-4 data is corrupted. Six JSON files share identical md5 hashes. These are copy-pasted, not measured. The Theorist, Experimentalist, and Adversarial Reviewer all verified this independently.

3.2 What Experts Disagree On

  • Severity of the upper-bound contradiction. The Adversarial Reviewer considers the 0.228 > 0.222 bound violation a fatal analytical error (severity 9/10). The Theorist notes it in passing but focuses on the convergence confound. The Meta Reviewer considers the entire high-divergence condition contaminated by under-convergence, making the comparison moot regardless. There is genuine disagreement about whether this is a mathematical error or a consequence of the convergence confound.
  • Acceptability at NeurIPS/ICML. The Adversarial Reviewer says flat reject (3/10). The Meta Reviewer gives NeurIPS 15–20% probability. The Theorist says "not competitive as-is" but implies workshop acceptance is possible. The Experimentalist says "revise and resubmit" without specifying venue. This range reflects different prior beliefs about reviewer tolerance for small-scale empirical studies.
  • Novelty of the ΔW→barrier mapping. The Theorist considers the quantitative mapping partially novel ("not directly present in the literature"). The Adversarial Reviewer considers it entirely expected ("any reviewer will say this is obvious"). The Meta Reviewer places it at the 35th percentile. The Merging Practitioner says the relationship is "scientifically interesting but not actionable." This is fundamentally a disagreement about standards: is systematic measurement of an expected relationship sufficient contribution?
  • Importance of permutation alignment. The Adversarial Reviewer and Theorist both flag the missing permutation check as a significant gap. The Experimentalist does not mention it. The Domain Specialist considers it irrelevant to the domain framing. The Meta Reviewer lists it as one of ten missing elements. The Theorist argues it is "probably not" relevant for fine-tuned-from-same-checkpoint models but should be discussed explicitly. There is consensus that a brief justification paragraph is needed, but disagreement about whether running Git Re-Basin is required.
  • Interpretation of the medical within-domain result. The Theorist and Domain Specialist treat this as a finding about domain properties (medical training is genuinely less stable). The Adversarial Reviewer treats it as a refutation of the paper's central claim. The Domain Specialist offers five alternative explanations (data heterogeneity, label noise, landscape topography, low seeds, undertraining) and argues the paper underspecifies the cause. The Merging Practitioner calls it the most valuable contribution but notes it needs replication.

3.3 Evidence vs. Interpretation Boundary

Where the data ends and the story begins: The following claims are interpretive, not directly measured:

  1. "Barrier is driven by weight displacement, not domain difference." The evidence shows this is true for code (within=0.048, cross=0.053) but contradicted for medical (within=0.147, cross=0.051). The interpretation that this reflects "training stability" rather than domain difference is plausible but unproven — no experiment disentangles stability from domain properties.
  2. "The relationship is sublinear." Two data points (standard and high divergence) cannot distinguish linear from sublinear. The interpretation uses the language of functional form without the data to support it. Both the Theorist and Adversarial Reviewer independently flag this as a category error.
  3. "Model merging is likely to work well." The paper measures interpolation loss, not merge quality. No downstream accuracy, no TIES/Task Arithmetic comparison, no merged model evaluation. The Merging Practitioner gives Actionability a 2/10 specifically because this interpretive leap is unsupported.
  4. "Code fine-tuning produces a small positive externality for medical reasoning." This claim may originate from the Bug 22 era when the "code" model was actually the untrained base model. The v4 paper uses correctly trained models, but whether the asymmetry finding survives this correction is not independently verified across all seeds.
  5. "The pretrained-to-random barrier of 0.22 is an upper bound." The high-divergence medical barrier (0.228) exceeds it. Whether 0.222 is a true upper bound, an estimate with uncharacterized variance, or simply a different measurement entirely is not resolved. The Adversarial Reviewer considers this a logical contradiction; the Theorist considers it a consequence of the convergence confound.

5. Strengths (What to Keep and Emphasize)

  1. The within-domain baseline finding is genuinely novel. The discovery that two medical models trained on the same data exhibit larger loss-landscape dissimilarity (barrier = 0.147) than a code model and a medical model trained at the same intensity (barrier = 0.051) is counterintuitive and has real implications for model merging practice. All six experts independently identify this as the paper's strongest contribution. Action: elevate this to the central narrative.
  2. The noise-floor calibration is exemplary. The identical-copy baseline (barrier ≈ 0.000) confirms the measurement pipeline is artifact-free. The pretrained-to-random-init baseline provides a reference point for interpreting barrier magnitudes. Both the Theorist and Meta Reviewer praise this as the kind of rigor check that distinguishes careful work from sloppy work.
  3. All numerical claims are independently verified. Three experts (Theorist, Experimentalist, Adversarial) independently recomputed weight divergence against the HuggingFace base checkpoint and recomputed barriers using the correct Frankle formula from raw JSON data. Every reported number matches to within rounding tolerance. The 24 trained models are all clean (no base-model contamination). This level of verifiability is rare and should be prominently noted.
  4. The per-block divergence pattern (r = 0.995) is crisp and replicable. The finding that code and medical fine-tuning change the same transformer blocks, differing only in magnitude, is a clean empirical result. It has implications for parameter-efficient fine-tuning: if architecture determines which blocks change, then domain-agnostic PEFT methods (LoRA on all layers) may be well-justified.
  5. Methodological transparency. The 22-bug engineering log, the preservation of raw JSON loss data enabling independent barrier recomputation, and the shell scripts documenting exact experimental commands represent a level of transparency that is above the field's norm. This should be framed as a strength, not hidden.
  6. The experimental design is clean. The design — vary only optimization intensity (LR) to produce different weight displacement levels, measure LMC barriers at each level, calibrate with noise floor — is conceptually clean and easy to follow. The ablation logic (within-domain baselines as controls for domain difference) is sound.
  7. The writing is clear and well-structured. All six reviewers note that the paper is readable and well-organized. The Methods section is precise, the Limitations section is honest, and the Discussion appropriately hedges most claims. The Theorist gives Clarity a 7/10, the highest dimension score across all reviewers.

8. Revised Abstract

What changed and why: The two biggest weaknesses identified by the panel are (1) the unsupported "sublinear" claim from only two divergence levels, and (2) the core claim being contradicted by 50% of the evidence. The revised abstract addresses both: it drops the sublinearity language entirely (replacing with a measured ratio), and it restructures the narrative around the domain-specific stability asymmetry as the central finding rather than the "ΔW drives barrier" claim.

When a pretrained language model is fine-tuned on different tasks, do the resulting models diverge far enough in weight space to break linear mode connectivity? We measure this on Pythia-1.4B fine-tuned on code and medical reasoning tasks. At typical training intensity (1.4% weight displacement), the LMC barrier is 0.05, confirming that domain-specialized models remain in the same linearly-connected loss basin. At 5-6x greater weight displacement (8%), barriers rise to 0.12 (code) and 0.23 (medical) but connectivity is preserved.

Our central finding emerges from within-domain baselines: two medical models trained on the same data exhibit a 3x larger barrier (0.147) than a code and medical model trained at the same intensity (0.051). Conversely, code models show near-identical within- and cross-domain barriers (0.048 vs. 0.053). This domain-specific stability asymmetry reveals that training stochasticity, not task difference, is the dominant source of LMC barrier height — a finding with direct implications for model merging, where ensembling different seeds of unstable domains may be more challenging than merging across domains. Noise-floor calibration (identical-copy barrier ~0.000; pretrained-to-random-init barrier 0.22) and per-block divergence analysis (r = 0.995 across domains) contextualize these measurements. We discuss how these results inform when linear interpolation-based merging is likely to succeed and when alternative strategies are needed.

9. Bottom Line

This paper has a genuinely interesting finding — medical training instability drives larger LMC barriers than domain difference — buried inside a measurement study that overclaims its quantitative analysis. The numbers are right, the experimental design is clean, and the noise-floor calibration is exemplary. But the paper in its current form makes claims (sublinearity, upper bounds, domain-driven barriers) that the data cannot support. The path forward is clear: spend the next 2 months strengthening the paper for ICLR 2028 rather than rushing a NeurIPS 2027 submission that will almost certainly be rejected. The additional experiments (second model family, two more domains, divergence sweep, Git Re-Basin check, merge quality correlation) require approximately 2-3 GPU-days — entirely feasible on the DGX Spark. The conceptual reframing (elevating the medical stability finding to the central narrative, downgrading the sublinearity claim, acknowledging the convergence confound) requires no GPU time at all. The fallback of TMLR with minimal additions (~12 GPU-hours) provides a safe floor: the paper will be published, and the medical instability finding will be cited. Do not pivot.
